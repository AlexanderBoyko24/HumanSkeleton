Overview of the Upper Limb Anatomy and Function

The upper limb, also known as the upper extremity, is the anatomical region of the body comprising the arms, forearms, wrists, and hands. It plays a crucial role in various activities such as manipulation, grasping, lifting, and performing fine motor tasks. Here's an overview of the upper limb:

Bones:

The upper limb consists of several bones, including the clavicle, scapula, humerus, radius, ulna, carpals, metacarpals, and phalanges. These bones provide structure, support, and attachment points for muscles, ligaments, and tendons.

Joints:

The upper limb contains several joints that enable movement and flexibility. These include the shoulder joint (glenohumeral joint), elbow joint, wrist joint, and joints of the hand and fingers.The shoulder joint is a ball-and-socket joint formed by the articulation of the humerus with the glenoid cavity of the scapula. It allows for a wide range of motion but sacrifices stability for mobility.The elbow joint is a hinge joint formed by the articulation of the humerus with the radius and ulna. It primarily allows for flexion and extension of the forearm.The wrist joint is a complex joint formed by the articulation of the radius, ulna, and carpal bones. It allows for flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, and circumduction of the hand.The joints of the hand and fingers include the metacarpophalangeal joints, proximal interphalangeal joints, and distal interphalangeal joints. These joints enable intricate movements such as grasping, pinching, and fine motor control.

Muscles:

The upper limb is surrounded by numerous muscles that originate from the axial skeleton (trunk) and insert into the bones of the upper limb. These muscles are responsible for movements of the shoulder, arm, forearm, wrist, and hand. Important muscle groups include the deltoid, rotator cuff muscles, biceps brachii, triceps brachii, flexor and extensor muscles of the forearm, intrinsic muscles of the hand, and muscles controlling finger movements.

Nerves:

The upper limb receives innervation from various nerves originating from the brachial plexus, a network of nerves formed by the anterior rami of spinal nerves C5-T1. Major nerves include the median nerve, ulnar nerve, radial nerve, and musculocutaneous nerve. These nerves provide sensory and motor innervation to the muscles and skin of the upper limb.

Vessels:

The upper limb is supplied with blood by branches of the subclavian artery, which becomes the axillary artery in the axilla and then gives rise to branches such as the brachial artery, radial artery, and ulnar artery. Venous drainage is provided by superficial and deep veins that accompany the arteries and drain into the axillary vein and subclavian vein. Overall, the upper limb is a complex and versatile structure that allows humans to perform a wide range of activities essential for daily living and interaction with the environment.

The Bones of the Upper Limbs

Clavicle

The clavicle is an S-shaped bone located horizontally at the base of the neck, connecting the sternum (breastbone) to the scapula (shoulder blade).

It serves as a strut to keep the arm away from the top of the thorax, allowing greater freedom of movement.

It also protects nerves and blood vessels passing below it.

Scapula

The scapula is a triangular-shaped bone situated on the posterior aspect of the thorax.

It articulates with the clavicle and humerus, forming the shoulder joint.

TThe scapula provides attachment points for various muscles that control movements of the shoulder and arm.


Humerus

The humerus is the long bone of the upper arm, extending from the shoulder to the elbow.

It articulates with the scapula at the shoulder joint and with the radius and ulna at the elbow joint.

The humerus serves as the attachment site for muscles involved in shoulder and arm movements.


Radius

The radius is one of the two bones of the forearm, located on the lateral (thumb) side of the forearm.

It extends from the lateral side of the elbow to the wrist.

The radius is involved in forearm rotation and articulates with the humerus and ulna.


Ulna

The ulna is the other bone of the forearm, located on the medial (pinky finger) side of the forearm.

It runs parallel to the radius and is slightly longer.

The ulna forms the elbow joint with the humerus and is involved in forearm rotation and stability.


Carpal Bones:

The carpal bones are a group of eight small bones arranged in two rows within the wrist.

These bones allow for the complex movements of the wrist joint.

The carpal bones include the scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform, trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, and hamate.


Metacarpal Bones:

TThe metacarpal bones are five long bones located in the palm of the hand, extending from the wrist to the base of the fingers.

They provide structure and support to the palm and contribute to the flexibility and movement of the hand.


Phalanges:

The phalanges are the bones of the fingers and thumb.

Each finger has three phalanges: proximal, middle, and distal.

The thumb has only two phalanges: proximal and distal.

Altogether, there are fourteen phalanges in each hand, allowing for fine movements and manipulation.
